Proofread: Check for errors, Obvious outliers, Typos Empty (null) rows. Ensure that columns are formatted as data types that correspond to how you will use their data. Years as number not date Remove introductory text such as titles or legends which might appear apart from your column headers Avoid spaces, merged cells and special chars in column headers. For example, age (months) might be age_months; “birthday” could be “birthdate”. Use consistent categories and units  qualitative or quantitative.

If using data from multiple sources, ensure that column names are the same for the same types of data For example, if the data for “year of birth,” "birthday," and "year" are the same type of data, they should be defined as a single term such as “birthyear” in all your data sets. This consistency is especially important for joining and analyzing data sets that are derived from multiple sources or time periods Data cleaning is 60% of you Create or Save structured data from spreadsheets in plain formats such as .CSV (comma separated values) Files from word processing software (e.g. Word) contain invisible formatting instructions that may later become mixed with your real data. Archiving:

Think of the future...

  1. Digital media lifespan

  2. Born digital records
Once your research is complete and published, your data will still exist. Knowing what to do with your data is part of your research journey and responsibility. As you are creating your data, consider questions around data archiving strategies. CD (2 - 5 years) USB (5 - 10 years) Hard Drives (3 - 5 years) It’s a good practice to have data in multiple digital media in case if one fails. For example, students can practice the ‘3-2-1’ backup strategy which advises one to have 3 copies of your data on two different media with one copy off-site for disaster recovery. We all heard stories of students losing their data because their computer crashed or they lost their USB stick. While students can implement long-term, digital preservation practices, nothing can last forever. The lifespan of digital media may depend on the number of accesses, write cycles, physical conditions, and storage. For example, a lifespan of a hard drive can last 3 to 5 years before some component fails. Another option students can do is to store their data in the cloud as long as they are not breaking any policies. Even born digital records can pose problems. If students use a proprietary software to create data, they have to keep in mind that information can be lost if the file is corrupted, the new version of the software cannot support the old file format for upload or to make any changes, and if the company that created the software no longer exists then you may not be able to download the software due to unavailability.
