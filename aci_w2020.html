Data Licenses:

Open Data

Licensed/ Proprietary Data

Open data is defined by the gvt of canada as structured data that is machine-readable, freely shared, used and built on without restrictions.
The key things to remember about open data are:
Availability and access: the data must be available as a whole and at no more than a reasonable reproduction cost, preferably by downloading over the internet. The data must also be available in a convenient and modifiable form.
Re-use and redistribution: the data must be provided under terms that permit re-use and redistribution including the intermixing with other datasets.
Universal participation: everyone must be able to use, re-use and redistribute. There should be no discrimination against fields of endeavour or against persons or groups. For example, 'non-commercial' restrictions that would prevent 'commercial' use, or restrictions of use for certain purposes (e.g. only in education), are not allowed.
Not all data can be open data. Sometimes an organization is unable to release certain data as open data because of certain restrictions.
  1. Privacy: A dataset or information that contains personal information about an individual
  2. Security: Information or data that may pose security risks to the institution, to the government, or to vulnerable or targeted individuals
  3. Confidentiality: Information or data that impairs the government’s ability to make some decisions
  4. Legacy information or data: Sometimes there is a substantive cost to making the resource eligible for release (for example, digitizing the resource, formatting it, ensuring it’s accessible)
  5. Legal and contractual limitations: A dataset may be subject to legal or contractual agreements that prevent it from being released. Agreements may include:
    1. limitations in data sharing agreements and memoranda of understanding
    2. third party data - government organizations that collect data for federal use, but where the federal organization may not have the rights to publish it on open.canada.ca
    3. commercial license - data purchased from third parties, which may have limited rights for distribution
    4. vendor limitations – data that is delivered under a contract, which captures exclusions for extracting and manipulating data
    5. non-disclosure agreements
    6. solicitor-client privilege
(https://open.canada.ca/en/open-data-principles)
Proprietary data refers to data that is controlled or owned by an organization. It's protected by copyright, trade secret laws, and even a patent agreement. Accessing this data usually means paying a sum of money and entering into an agreement with the license holder.
